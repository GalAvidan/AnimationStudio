# Script: Why the Sky Is Blue — General Audience

## Working Title

Why the Sky Is Blue

## Audience

Curious adults with no science background. They've wondered this before but never looked it up. They should leave with a clear, memorable answer they can repeat to someone else.

## Target Runtime

~60 seconds

## Tone

Warm, calm, visually-led. Let the animation carry the explanation; narration stays short and concrete. No jargon — when a term appears, it earns its place.

---

## Script

Why is the sky blue?

[VISUAL: A deep blue sky fills the frame. A bright sun rises slowly from the lower-left corner. The question appears as clean white type, then fades.]

---

Sunlight looks white — but it's every color at once.

[VISUAL: A single white ray enters from the upper right. It fans open into a full color spectrum — red, orange, yellow, green, blue, violet — spreading across the frame.]
[SYNC: spectrum-fan — the spectrum fans open on the word "every"]
[TEXT: caption “Sunlight looks white — but it’s every color at once.” appears with the white ray; zone label “Visible light spectrum” appears when the fan opens]

---

Earth's atmosphere is full of tiny gas molecules — mostly nitrogen and oxygen.

[VISUAL: The view pulls back to show Earth's curve and a thin, glowing atmosphere band. Small dot-shaped particles appear scattered throughout the layer.]
[TEXT: zone labels “Space” / “← Atmosphere →” / “Earth” identify the three regions; caption “Earth’s atmosphere is full of tiny gas molecules.” appears in the space region; “N₂ · O₂” label appears near the molecules]

---

When sunlight hits those molecules, shorter wavelengths scatter — in every direction.

Blue light bounces. Red light passes straight through.

[VISUAL: Zoom into a single molecule. Colored rays approach from one side. Red and orange continue past undisturbed. Blue explodes outward in all directions. Pull back: dozens of molecules repeat the same behavior, filling the atmosphere with scattered blue light.]
[SYNC: scatter-bounce — the blue burst fires on "bounces"]
[TEXT: zone label “Single molecule”; stage-1 caption “Shorter wavelengths scatter in every direction.” with the incoming rays; swaps to “Blue bounces · Red passes through” at the scatter-bounce sync point]

---

Violet actually scatters even more than blue. So why not a violet sky?

Our eyes are simply better tuned for blue.

[VISUAL: A soft human-vision sensitivity curve overlays the spectrum. The blue peak glows bright. Violet is present but visibly dimmer. The violet portion of the spectrum fades back; blue holds its intensity.]
[TEXT: stage-1 caption “Violet actually scatters even more than blue.” appears with the spectrum bands; swaps to “Our eyes are simply better tuned for blue.” when violet dims]

---

At sunrise or sunset, light takes a longer path through the atmosphere.

By the time it reaches you, the blue has already scattered away — leaving orange and red.

[VISUAL: The sun sits at the horizon. A shallow, long path traces through the thick lower atmosphere. Blue scatters out along the way, depleted before it arrives. The remaining light — warm reds and oranges — reaches an observer on the ground.]
[SYNC: long-path — the path line extends on "longer path"]
[TEXT: stage-1 caption “At sunrise and sunset, light travels a much longer path.” appears at beat start; zone label “← longer path through atmosphere” appears on the path at the sync point; swaps to “Blue has already scattered away.” after blue depletes]

---

The sky is blue because blue light can't stop bouncing.

[VISUAL: Pull back to a wide view of the sky. Countless scattered blue rays converge from every direction toward a single silhouetted observer on open ground. The sky is a deep, even blue — exactly what you'd see looking up.]
